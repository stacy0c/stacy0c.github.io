Dissertation

Organizing Language Services: The Impact of Healthcare Consolidation and State Policies on Access and Quality for Limited-English Proficient Patients

Committee: Hector Rodriguez (Chair), Jenny Guadamuz, Becky Staiger, Amanda Brewster

In March 2025, an executive order revoked a 2000 federal directive requiring agencies to improve language access for limited English proficient (LEP) individuals — raising the stakes of understanding what organizational and policy conditions sustain interpreter services for this population. Chen's dissertation examines how hospital consolidation reshapes the interpreter services workforce and whether state Medicaid reimbursement policy can serve as a lever to expand language access and improve health equity for LEP patients.

The three papers move in a deliberate progression: from organizational mechanism (how consolidation restructures who controls interpreter services) to consequence (how that restructuring affects patient safety outcomes for LEP patients) to policy impact (whether Medicaid reimbursement reform, in the presence of an adequate interpreter workforce, improves preventive care uptake). Research Interests

Organizations & Management Hospital consolidation · Language services · ACOs
›

Organizations and management research forms the core of Chen's dissertation. Her work examines how hospital mergers and acquisitions reshape the interpreter services workforce — and through that workforce, care quality for LEP patients. Using 15 years of AHA Annual Survey data on 4,660 hospitals, Chen found that consolidation centralizes interpreter services (a 10.5 percentage-point shift toward system- or network-contracted arrangements) without expanding overall availability; acquired hospitals are no more likely to offer services than those that remain independent. A second paper, currently underway, follows the chain to patient outcomes — linking AHA data to HCUP State Inpatient Databases to examine AHRQ Patient Safety Indicators and readmissions for LEP-proxied patients before and after acquisition.

Beyond the dissertation, Chen has studied how primary care practices configure language services and how financial incentives for physician groups and accountable care organizations shape racial equity in ambulatory care quality.

Medicaid Policy & Coverage Reimbursement reform · Income effects · Language access
›

Chen's research examines how Medicaid policy shapes both health access and economic well-being. Dissertation Paper 3 asks whether state Medicaid reimbursement policies for interpreter services — adopted by 18 states and DC with staggered rollout from 2006 to 2022 — increase preventive care uptake among LEP adults. Using restricted MEPS-NHIS data (~46,000 LEP adults) and a Callaway–Sant'Anna staggered difference-in-differences design, the paper tests whether these policies translate into more influenza vaccinations, cancer screenings, and fewer delayed care episodes. A key hypothesis is that effects are moderated by local interpreter workforce supply: reimbursement reforms shift the financial incentive to hire interpreters, but only translate into improved care where an adequate workforce exists to respond.

In separate work with Becky Staiger, Chen used IRS administrative tax data to show that gaining Medicaid eligibility increased income by 9.6% for newly eligible adults — through effects on earnings, tax credits, and unpaid time off work. This was the first study to examine the ACA Medicaid expansion's direct impact on income.

Immigrant Health Outcomes & Inequities Coverage · Access · Community health
›

Chen collaborates with Jenny Guadamuz on a program of research examining how citizenship and immigration status shape healthcare access, coverage, and health outcomes. Recent work evaluated California's Medi-Cal expansion for undocumented adults and its effects on insurance coverage among noncitizens, and examines how citizenship status mediates cancer screening disparities. Earlier community-based participatory research examined health priorities, behaviors, and service utilization among Brazilian immigrant women in the Boston area — producing findings that directly informed the design of culturally responsive community interventions.
