Dissertation

Organizing Language Services: The Impact of Healthcare Consolidation and State Policies on Access and Quality for Limited-English Proficient Patients

Committee: Hector Rodriguez (Chair), Jenny Guadamuz, Becky Staiger, Amanda Brewster

Research Interests

Organizations & Management Hospital consolidation · Language services · ACOs
›

Organizations and management research forms the core of Stacy's dissertation. Her work examines how hospital mergers and acquisitions impact care quality for LEP patients. Stacy has found that consolidation centralizes interpreter services without expanding overall availability. A second paper, currently underway, examines patient outcomes to examine AHRQ Patient Safety Indicators and readmissions.

Beyond the dissertation, she has studied how primary care practices configure language services and how financial incentives for physician groups and accountable care organizations shape racial equity in ambulatory care quality.

Medicaid Policy & Coverage Reimbursement reform · Income effects · Language access
›

Stacy's research examines how Medicaid policy shapes both health access and economic well-being. Dissertation Paper 3 asks whether state Medicaid reimbursement policies for interpreter services increase preventive care uptake among LEP adults.

In separate work with Stacy used IRS administrative tax data to show that gaining Medicaid eligibility increased income by 9.6% for newly eligible adults — through effects on earnings, tax credits, and unpaid time off work.

Immigrant Health Outcomes & Inequities Coverage · Access · Community health
›

Stacy examines how citizenship and immigration status shape healthcare access, coverage, and health outcomes. Recent work evaluated California's Medi-Cal expansion for undocumented adults and its effects on insurance coverage among noncitizens, and examines how citizenship status mediates cancer screening disparities. Earlier community-based participatory research examined health priorities, behaviors, and service utilization among Brazilian immigrant women.
